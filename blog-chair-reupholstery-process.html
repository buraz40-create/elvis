The Chair Reupholstery Process — Every Stage Explained

Published December 27, 2012  ·  Elvis Upholstery & Draperies  ·  ← Back to Blog

Clients frequently ask us what actually happens between dropping off their chair and picking it up again. The answer is more involved than most people expect — and understanding the process helps explain why quality reupholstery takes the time it takes, and why the result is so dramatically different from a superficial re-cover. Here is a complete, honest walkthrough of every stage in our chair reupholstery process at Elvis Upholstery & Draperies, Atlantic Beach, FL.

Stage 1: Intake and Frame Assessment

Every chair that enters our workshop is assessed before work begins. We examine the frame condition — joints, corner blocks, legs, tacking rails — and identify any structural repairs needed before upholstery can proceed. We assess the existing spring and webbing condition, determine the foam specification required for the piece's intended use, and review any special requirements of the chosen fabric (pattern matching, directional pile, contrast welt). A written scope of work is agreed with the client before we start.

Stage 2: Complete Strip-Down

The chair is stripped completely — all the way to bare wood. Every layer is removed: dust cover, outside back, outside arms, inside back, inside arms, seat platform, and all decorative trim. This step is not optional. Reupholstering over old padding or deteriorated foam produces inferior results and a shorter lifespan. The only way to do this correctly is to start from the frame.

Stage 3: Frame Repair and Preparation

With the frame exposed, any necessary repairs are completed. Loose joints are re-glued and clamped — typically overnight. Broken corner blocks are replaced. Tacking rails are repaired or reinforced where wood has split or compressed from previous stapling. The frame is inspected once more before we proceed to the next stage.

Stage 4: Webbing, Springs and Foundation

New webbing is applied to the seat deck and back — stretched to the correct tension, interlaced in both directions, and stapled securely at each rail. Springs (sinuous or coil, depending on the original specification) are inspected and re-tied or replaced as needed. This foundation work is invisible in the finished piece but determines how the chair will feel and perform for the next 15–20 years.

Stage 5: Foam Cutting and Padding

Foam is cut by hand to the precise shape of every element: seat platform, inside back, arm pads, and any additional cushion elements. Density and ILD (Indentation Load Deflection) rating are specified to match the piece's use — a daily-use family room chair requires different foam than a formal occasional chair that is rarely sat in. Foam is adhered to the surface and then wrapped in a layer of Dacron batting to produce the characteristic soft, rounded profile of a professionally upholstered piece.

Stage 6: Fabric Cutting and Application

The fabric is laid out with every cut planned: pattern repeats centred across the key faces, directional pile running consistently, every element cut with adequate seam allowance. Inside elements are upholstered first — inside back, inside arms, seat — before the outside elements are applied. Fabric is pulled to consistent, even tension along every rail. Welt cord seams are sewn, pressed, and hand-tacked at every visible seam line.

Stage 7: Final Finishing and Inspection

Decorative legs are fitted. Nailhead trim is applied where specified. The dust cover is hand-tacked to the underside. The finished piece is then inspected against our quality checklist — if anything falls short of our standard, it is corrected before the piece leaves the workshop. Only then is the client contacted for pickup or delivery.

The Elvis Upholstery Standard

Every piece we produce passes through all seven stages above. There are no shortcuts, and no piece leaves our Atlantic Beach workshop that we are not proud of. That consistency — maintained across 27 years and hundreds of commissions — is the foundation of our reputation on the First Coast.
